Chemical Kinetics & Combustion

Developing reduced kinetic mechanisms for fuel combustion and pollutant formation

Compact n-Dodecane Kinetic Mechanism for Soot Prediction

Development of a compact n-dodecane kinetic mechanism using path flux analysis and artificial neural networks (ANN) to predict soot formation in diesel and jet fuel surrogates. The mechanism serves as a computational tool for understanding pollutant formation in non-premixed flames.

Key Achievements

  • Developed 155-species, 827-reaction mechanism with PAH sub-models
  • Achieved 5.5× computational speedup compared to detailed mechanisms
  • Extended prediction capability from 5 to 16 species including aromatics
  • Validated against experimental data for ignition delay and flame speed
  • Successfully coupled with CFD for spatial distribution analysis

This research employs hybrid reduction methods combining traditional sensitivity analysis with machine learning techniques to create efficient yet accurate kinetic models for practical combustion applications.

CFD Investigation of PAH and Soot Formation in Diffusion Flames

Computational fluid dynamics study examining polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon (PAH) formation and soot volume fraction in methane-dodecane blended non-premixed flames, revealing detailed reaction pathways and spatial distributions of aromatics.

Research Highlights

  • Investigated spatial distributions of single-ring to four-ring aromatics
  • Performed rate of production (ROP) analysis for aromatic formation pathways
  • Accurate prediction of soot volume fractions in co-flow diffusion flames
  • Analyzed effects of fuel blending on pollutant emissions
  • Validated CFD predictions against experimental measurements

The study provides insights into fuel-to-pollutant conversion processes, essential for designing cleaner combustion systems in automotive and aerospace applications.

Thermal Management Systems

Optimizing heat transfer performance through advanced manifold and microchannel designs

CFD Analysis of Microchannel Heat Sink with Different Manifold Arrangements

Computational investigation of heat transfer characteristics in microchannel heat sinks (MCHS) with three different manifold configurations: Rectangular (R), Rectangle with Semi-Circular (RSC), and Divergent-Convergent (DC) designs.

Key Findings

  • Divergent-Convergent manifold showed 9-10% higher heat transfer coefficient
  • Tested across Reynolds number range of 342-857 with water as working fluid
  • Analyzed performance at multiple power inputs (50W, 75W, 100W, 125W)
  • DC design achieved superior Nusselt number compared to R and RSC types
  • Optimized channel dimensions: 500μm width, 3000μm depth, 24.5mm length

This research addresses the critical need for efficient cooling in high-power electronics, providing design guidelines for next-generation microchannel heat exchangers.

Manifold Design Optimization for Enhanced Cooling Performance

Experimental and computational study investigating the relationship between manifold geometry, flow distribution, and thermal performance in microchannel heat sinks. Focus on optimizing inlet/outlet configurations for uniform flow distribution and maximum heat dissipation.

Research Contributions

  • Demonstrated 10% improvement in heat transfer with optimized manifold design
  • Reduced pressure drop while maintaining high Nusselt numbers
  • Investigated effects of channel aspect ratio on thermal performance
  • Developed design correlations for manifold optimization
  • Applicable to electronics cooling, automotive, and aerospace systems

Results provide practical guidelines for designing high-performance microchannel heat exchangers with improved flow uniformity and reduced manufacturing complexity.
